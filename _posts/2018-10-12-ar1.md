In [December of 2016][0] I started work on a Python3 port of some [Julia
code][1] that I had been writing to help me understand a pre-print of a paper
written by my former Electronics lecturer at Glasgow University, [Dr John
Williamson][2]. The paper in question is, by John's own admission, completely
mind bending, in part due to the fantastic picture of the world around us that
it paints (aiming to derive the properties of matter from light) but also due to
its use of Clifford Algebra as the language of the theory.


# Clifford The Big Red Algebra
As with most new topics I try to learn, on hearing reading about Clifford
Algebras in the paper (and <i>immediately</i> feeling out of my depth) I turned
to Wikipedia:

> In mathematics, a Clifford algebra is an algebra generated by a vector space
> with a quadratic form, and is a unital associative algebra. As K-algebras,
> they generalize the real numbers, complex numbers, quaternions and several
> other hypercomplex number systems.

Start simple, build up to being able to understand the paper.

With that in mind, the first thing to get my head round was the rules of the
game. As that daunting first paragraph from Wikipedia explained, Clifford
Algebra isn't actually one thing: it's a category of things. One way to think of
numbers systems ([Real numbers][3], [Complex Numbers][4], [Quaternions][5]...)
is in terms of their geometric properties.

## Number Lines & Argand Diagrams
If you think back to school and when you first learned to count, you most likely
were familiar with the idea of a number line. You probably had one on the wall
of your classroom. They look something like this (minus the square roots and
Greek letters at primary school of course):

<div style="text-align:center">
  <img src ="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Real_number_line.svg/497px-Real_number_line.svg.png"/>
</div>

The concept is pretty straight forward: if you <i>"add"</i> you move right along
the line and if you <i>"subtract"</i> you move left. We all accepted this without
question and happily moved on with our lives.

Yay for maths. (That is, until you start trying to subtract negative numbers...)

But what does it mean to move left and right on our number line? Well, one way
to look at it (and the way that we'll be looking at it) is to say that normal,
every day <i>Real</i> numbers are <b>One Dimensional</b>. That is to say, we can
always put them somewhere on our number line: the only concern is how far along
we have to go to the left or right.

In other words, they give us one piece of information: <i>How far are we from 0?</i>

What if we want two pieces of information? Well, then we're into the familiar
secondary school maths lesson territory of Cartesian (x, y) coordinates and
graphs:

<div style="text-align:center">
  <img src ="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Cartesian-coordinate-system.svg/354px-Cartesian-coordinate-system.svg.png"/>
</div>

But now we have two numbers, not one. If we want to still be able to use only
one number to describe a point then we need to move into the world of Complex
Numbers.

## Don't panic! This is going to be fine.

Complex numbers are, at their simplest, nothing more than a way of taking your
familiar (x, y) coordinates from school and wrapping them up in a set of rules
that let you treat them like single numbers. Now, admittedly in doing so,
mathematicians confused everyone with the spectacularly poorly named concept of
<i>imaginary</i> numbers, which has caused hundreds of years of misconceptions
around what, at its heart, is really quite a simple idea.

You could as easily call them <i>horizontal</i> and <i>vertical</i> numbers (or
<i>latitudinal</i> and <i>longitudinal</i> numbers if geography is your thing).
The important idea is that they point in <b>different directions</b>: just like
our X and Y axes from school.

<div style="text-align:center"><img src ="/assets/ar1/argand.gif"/></div>

### So if Real numbers are 1D, and Complex numbers are 2D, what do 3D numbers look like?
<br>
Well, in 1843, after a lot of tinkering and hard work, Irish mathematician
William Hamilton devised a system of numbers that can be used to describe
rotations in three dimensions. Much to his surprise, he needed four components
to get his new numbers to work, not three (giving them the name [Quaternions][5]
as a result).

### A quick aside on components
<br>
By components, I mean that our one "number" is made up of a set of capital R
<i>Real</i> numbers added together. You might be familiar with the idea of
<i>i</i> being the "imaginary" number and with Complex numbers being written (as
in the diagram above) as something like <b>3 + 2i</b>.

This really is the same as saying we have coordinates of (3, 2) in the Real
and Imaginary axes, the same way that at school you would have said that (3, 2)
meant that you were "3 along the x-axis and 2 up the y-axis".

In the case of Quaternions we now have <i>i, j & k</i> as our "imaginary"
numbers but, importantly, they are not equal to one another. They are a nice
mathematical trick that let us describe different directions, the same way that
we can thing of Real/Imaginary as X/Y on our graph.

## AxB is not BxA
In addition to the added complexity of doubling the number of components (again),
Quaternions introduced several mind bending new ideas that turn multiplication
on its head.

The most important departure from the traditional idea of multiplying two
numbers is that, with Quaternions, it matters what order you multiply two
numbers together. We are all used to the idea that 4x5 is the same thing as 5x4,
but try the same thing with Quaternions and you'll find that you'll end up with
two different answers!

Great.

Well actually, it is great; if describing motion in three dimensional space is
what you are trying to do. The <i>system</i> becomes more complicated but it
also becomes more powerful, allowing us to describe things we couldn't do
before.

At this stage, you can probably see where this is going...


# Breaking Out The Squared Paper
As a former mathematics teacher, I have a deep fondness in the core of my being
for squared paper and a good cup of coffee. By this stage, I'd managed to get my
head around the idea that you can keep playing this trick of doubling the number
of components in you "number" and in return, gain the ability to describe shapes
and motions in a dimension one higher than before.

The trade off is that at each stage it gets harder and harder to multiply your
numbers together.

For normal, Real, numbers we just use the rules of multiplication we learned at
school. For Complex numbers things are a little harder but essentially we just
use the rules we already know for negative numbers:

- Real x Real = Real
- Real x Imaginary = Imaginary
- Imaginary x Real = Imaginary
- Imaginary x Imaginary = Real
